How to Price Septic Pumping Jobs: The Complete Guide

June 21, 2026 ● 5 min read

If you own a septic pumping business, you've probably had that sinking feeling after quoting a job — realizing you forgot to factor in the disposal fee, or you underestimated the drive time. Maybe you've even had a job where you worked harder than expected and barely broke even.

You're not alone. Most septic pumpers start out pricing by "gut feel," and most of them are leaving 10–15% on the table.

This guide breaks down exactly how to price septic pumping jobs — covering every cost, common mistakes, and a simple system to make sure you never underbid again.

The 5 Factors That Determine Septic Pumping Prices

Every septic pumping job has five cost buckets. Miss one, and your profit margin takes a hit.

1. Tank Size and Access

The size of the tank directly affects pump-out time. Standard residential tanks range from 500–1,500 gallons, while commercial tanks can hit 5,000+ gallons.

  • 500–750 gallon tank: 15–25 minutes pump time
  • 1,000–1,500 gallon tank: 25–45 minutes pump time
  • 2,000+ gallon tank: 45–90+ minutes pump time

Access matters too. Is the lid buried? Is the tank behind a fence? Do you need a helper to move debris? Add 15–30 minutes for difficult access.

Rule of thumb: $0.10–$0.25 per gallon for standard residential pumping, with a minimum trip charge of $150–$250.

2. Sludge Level and Tank Condition

A tank that hasn't been pumped in 5+ years has a thick sludge layer that takes longer to break up and pump out.

  • Normal maintenance (every 2–3 years): Standard rate
  • Heavy sludge (5+ years since last pump): Add 25–50%
  • Grease trap pumping: Higher rate due to thicker waste (typically $75–$150 surcharge)

3. Travel Time and Fuel

This is the cost most operators forget to calculate properly.

  • Within 10 miles: Include in base rate
  • 10–25 miles: Add $25–$50
  • 25–50 miles: Add $50–$100
  • 50+ miles: Add $100+, or decline the job

Real-world example: If you drive 45 minutes each way for a $350 pump job and spend $30 in fuel, you've just eaten 9% of your revenue before you even start working.

4. Disposal Fees

Disposal costs vary wildly by location. Some areas charge as little as $0.02 per gallon, others charge $0.08–$0.12 per gallon. A 1,000-gallon tank could cost you $20 in one county and $120 in the next.

5. Labor and Overhead

Your time is worth something.

  • Truck + labor cost: $100–$200 per hour (truck, insurance, wages)
  • Office overhead: 10–15% (scheduling, billing, marketing)
  • Profit margin target: 20–30% after all costs

The Most Common Pricing Mistakes

Mistake #1: Forgetting Disposal Fees

This is the #1 mistake. When disposal is $80 and you forgot to include it, you just donated $80 of profit to the treatment plant.

Mistake #2: Underestimating Drive Time

"That job is only 15 miles away" — but 15 miles in a heavy diesel truck takes fuel, time, and wear. Calculate round-trip time.

Mistake #3: Using the Same Price for Every Tank

A 500-gallon tank and a 1,500-gallon tank require completely different effort. Price by the gallon, not by the visit.

Mistake #4: Not Having a Minimum Charge

Set a minimum trip charge of $200–$300. Small jobs that don't cover your overhead are worse than no job at all.

Mistake #5: Guessing Instead of Calculating

One bad estimate can wipe out the profit from three good jobs.
